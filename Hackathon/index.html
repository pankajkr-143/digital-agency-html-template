In this online qualifying round, participants must submit a comprehensive presentation to showcase their project effectively.

Maintain professionalism, adhere to guidelines.

Note: Participants are required to use Intel Developer Cloud, irrespective of the chosen track. Projects incorporating the Intel oneAPI Developer toolkit will receive preference in the final evaluation, further highlighting the significance of Intel's technologies in this competition.

The Presentation must follow the guidelines mentioned below:

1. Title and Team Introduction:

● A compelling title that encapsulates the essence of your project.

● Mention LinkedIn and GitHub IDs of your team members.

Green Charcoal Hackathon

Power plants are the biggest consumers of coal. Typically a 1000 MW plant consumes about 5 million tonnes of coal annually. India's total coal-based power generation capacity is about 2 lakh MW, which theoretically can consume approximately 1000 million tonnes of coal annually.

Even 10% of that, if replaced with torrefied charcoal will amount to 100 million tonnes of this fuel which will require approximately 165 MTPA of agro residue (considering 60% yield), sufficient to wipe out the entire unused agro residue in the country and thus eliminating the farm fires and produce approx. 20,000 MW of renewable power. The idea has vast potential to increase rural income, provide local employment, cleaning the air by eliminating farm fires and also to generate enormous renewable energy with existing coal power infrastructure.

The technology to produce torrefied fuel using agro residue biomass is not easily accessible to small entrepreneurs due to the higher cost of imported machines, lack of sufficient manufacturers, and non-availability of the torrefaction plants having smaller capacity suitable for cottage industry entrepreneurs. To fast-track the development the technology for converting agro residue to charcoals, NVVN, a wholly owned subsidiary of NTPC limited, in partnership with EESL plans to organise a technology challenge Green Charcoal Hackathon. Smart Mobility Hackathon

Nearly half a million road accidents happen every year in India and over hundred and fifty thousand people die every year. Those numbers are comparable to some of the most major natural disasters or even epidemics. Yet road safety elicits less attention that it should. The brightest minds of our generation are making mobility awesome by working on electric vehicles but the other part of making mobility awesome is to end the needless physical harm on our roads.

How can we make our roads safer ? 

There are many ways of making our roads safer: prevention and mitigations. Prevention is where we prevent an accident, mitigation is where we do better handling of accidents. Both can make a difference to overall lives lost on roads. Within prevention :

We can make roads safer by making safer infrastructure (better light conditions, signage, roads etc.). Let’s call them ‘Where we drive’ factors.  

At another level we can make roads safer by making our vehicles safer - we could call them ‘What we drive’ factors

And lastly we could just drive well - the ‘how we drive’ factors.

Goes without saying that if we could improve the ‘how we drive’ - we can achieve safety at the least cost and at an individual level. ‘Where we drive’ & ‘What we drive’ is a much larger canvas of problem solving and needs many stakeholders to make a dent in the problem but ‘How we drive’ interventions can be driven faster. So we want to focus on them for this hackathon.

So how do we solve the problem of making our driving safer - how do we help people drive safer through awareness, through incentives, through gamification, how do we make sure that people are driving safely to start with. That would mean that they are driving within speed limits, not overtaking unnecessarily, braking slowly - so others can adjust, accelerate gently so others can figure out, keeping enough distance between their vehicles and so on.

For example: experts say that nearly a quarter of accidents happen because of distracted driving - with mobile usage a major part of the problem. So how do we stop people from using their mobile phones while driving ?
